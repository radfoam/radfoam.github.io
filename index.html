Abstract:

Radiance fields have revolutionized the field of 3D understanding from images. Early in their history, NeRF methods produced high quality models, but struggled to match the speeds required by computer graphics applications. Recently, the field has embraced the use of models based on splatting, which take advantage of the superior efficiency of rasterization, and achieve real-time speeds in inference. This arc of development mirrors the history of real-time computer graphics itself. A broad space of rendering methods and representations of geometry gave way to the uniform adoption of triangles and hardware rasterization, as these delivered the speeds needed for interactive applications. However, as algorithms and hardware have evolved, various rendering techniques based on ray tracing have once again become popular, as ray tracing allows for the natural expression of many light transport phenomena which are difficult or impossible to produce with rasterization. In this project, we aim to create similar opportunities for radiance fields by proposing a radiance field representation which admits a ray tracing algorithm efficient enough to challenge rasterization.

Our method works by filling space with a polyhedral open-cell foam constructed from a learnable, adaptively densified point set through the Voronoi diagram. By endowing each cell with the density and radiance properties of a radiance field, we define a model which can be volumetrically rendered in a differentiable manner. The process of rendering can then be accelerated by walking through the foam from cell to cell, crossing polyhedral faces which intersect the ray. We show that this yields fast rendering (between 150-180 FPS on the Mip-NeRF 360 dataset), while supporting the full range of features one would expect from a ray tracer, such as non-pinhole cameras and secondary reflection rays. We also completely avoid the dependence of competing real-time ray tracing methods on dedicated ray tracing hardware, as our method can be implemented efficiently on any GPU with modern programmable shading capabilities.
